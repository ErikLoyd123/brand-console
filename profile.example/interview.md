# Voice and Positioning Interview, Jordan Rivera

The raw, re-distillable source behind the voice card. Captured in Jordan's own words and polished into a faithful transcript (phrasings preserved, verbatim samples kept exact). The themes below are a backbone; the questions adapt to the answers.

## 1. Motivation and what winning looks like

Two reasons, and Jordan is honest about which is the real one.

**1. Staying invisible cost them.** On the roastery bar Jordan did the work and let the shop take the credit, which was fine until it was not. Winning a year from now is being known for the craft-and-data point of view, not for a logo.

**2. The real reason: the brand feeds RoastLog.** Jordan needs a personal following so RoastLog finds the home roasters who would actually use it. "I would happily never post, it does not give me joy, but it is good for the app and the app is mine, so it is the same thing." A means to an end, not a passion. The system must keep the lift small.

The deeper want is connection and learning: Jordan wants to compare notes with other roasters, not broadcast at them.

### Energy versus friction

**Friction:** self-promotion and being perceived. Jordan does not enjoy posting and never has. The system does the heavy lifting and asks for as little raw material as possible.

**Energy:** teaching a technique, pointing people at gear or beans they do not know about, and highlighting other roasters' wins. Lean here.

## 2. Real brewing and roasting opinions

Content seeds, each a potential post.

- **Seed A. Roast color lies about caffeine.** People read a dark roast as "stronger," but the number barely moves. The real variable is dose and extraction.
- **Seed B. Roasting by feel and logging the curve are two different skills** (the clean craft-and-data example). Many roasters can pull a great batch by feel but never log it, so they cannot repeat it. Many data-first roasters log everything and never taste against it, so the numbers mean nothing.
- **Seed C. Grind consistency beats grinder price** for most home setups, up to a point.
- **Seed D. Green coffee storage** quietly wrecks more cups than roast technique does.
- **Seed E. Most home espresso is ground too coarse** and pulled too fast, and the fix is cheaper than a new machine.

POV notes: Jordan explains the craft side and the data side in the same breath. Fair-minded even when critical (gives cheap gear its due). Highly concrete: real origins, real temperatures, real tasting notes. Generous, not corrective: "here is another way to taste this," not "here is what you are doing wrong."

## 3. Stories and the arc

The accountability origin story is the deepest theme. On the roastery bar Jordan had a head roaster who never let a scorched batch hide behind an excuse, and Jordan took that and ran with it.

A large reason Jordan left Harbor Roasters ties directly to it. They caught themself blaming the founder for where the shop was heading, thinking "if we just sourced differently, if they had not cut that corner." Jordan was senior enough to own it and did, for a long time, until they noticed they had stopped owning it. Recognizing that is ultimately why they left.

> Handling guardrail (hard rule): this story must always be framed as Jordan holding themself accountable, never as criticism of Harbor Roasters or its founder. Jordan still values those relationships and not criticizing them is a hard rule. Any draft that tilts toward blaming the former roastery fails the review gate.

The only comfortable self-story mode is the self-accountable one. Never the hero of their own story.

## 4. Voice mechanics

**Admired technique.** Jordan liked a wave of posts where a roaster told the story of a customer nailing a home batch, and you only saw the roaster's role if you read between the lines. This is the hero-is-someone-else pattern: make the other person the hero, let your own skill be inferred.

**Cringe list:**
- Coffee influencers who treat it like a lifestyle flex instead of a craft.
- People who cast themselves as the hero, especially loudly or often.

**Words and register:** plain and jargon-free, but still credible. Not hype, not a hard sell, not oversharing. Jordan flags "elevate" and "unlock" as words to avoid.

## 5. Audience

**Audience: do not narrow it.** Home roasters, cafe folks curious about roasting, gear tinkerers, green-buying clubs, and the small-shop owners who might use RoastLog.

**The relationship goal splits by context:**
- **Personal brand: no ask.** Personal content gives, teaches, and connects, full stop.
- **RoastLog: yes, an ask.** Product-adjacent content may lead to a try or a chat, one soft honest line at most.

Jordan genuinely likes the conversation. Replies and DMs are a primary mode, not an afterthought.

## 6. The catch / skeptic lens

What Jordan considers dishonest in the space: gear reviews that are really paid placements dressed as taste tests, and "one weird trick" roast advice that ignores the bean and the machine in front of you. Jordan wants to call out the placement-as-review move without punching down at the people who fell for it.
